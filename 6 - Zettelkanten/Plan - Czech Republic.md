# Getting hired as a programmer / developer in the Czech Republic (For foreigners)

I'm writing this post for those of you thinking of coming over to the Czech Republic in hope of finding work as a programmer. Or those of you who are already in the Czech Republic and perhaps looking for a new job.

This post is for foreigners only.

This post is written from my direct experiences, I've lived in this country for close to 8 years now. What I am about to say might not be completely politically correct - but it is the truth!

It might sound obvious but this ain't Kansas, Dorothy! The Czech Republic isn't exactly flooded with lucrative IT jobs. There are jobs here sure, but just by being foreign you're automatically cut off from a massive chunk of the job sector for 2 reasons:

1. Language! You're most likely English, You might even be native in Polish, Russian, Ukrainian or German, but unless you speak very good Czech (not Dobry Den, Dalsi pivo prosim) you can forget about working for a Czech only company * There are some exceptions here and there obviously.
    
2. Czechs can be inherently racist. Not all Czechs and not always maliciously. This might not be the politically correct thing to discuss but when it comes to finding work - it is pertinent to at least mention this. However having mentioned this, I am also required to back up this statement.
    

If you don't want to read this just skip this entire bullet point*

- While working for a company in Prague, the company acquired a new project to the value of 10 million crowns. This work had a deadline of 6 months. For this reason the MD was frantically looking for new developers. I made a recommendation - a friend of mine. He was very keen to see his CV. I was nearly certain the guy would get hired based on his experience and education. However when said MD noticed he was Polish he said to me "Oh but he's Polish!" and that was the end of it. I have too noticed a strange lack of interest when applying for work at companies that aren't multinationals, where I have 100% of the time failed to be invited for an interview. Let me say this again 100% of the time - Me and others around me (including Czechs) agree that this is because of my nationality. Lastly if you have any doubt at all, just use your common sense, Czechs are typically extremely patriotic and proud about their country and culture. Working with someone from another culture and language, for the most part is a pain in the ass to the Czech. Unless these Czechs work in a multinational themselves and speak English.
    

Ok So all I've really said so far is that you're excluded from a massive chunk of the job sector (realistically). So what does that leave?

Well the truly sad part is that cuts off nearly all of the cool startups. The companies that are just getting funded with cool ideas and give you breathing room to be creative, build products and innovate.

It does leave open multinationals operating in the Czech Republic, so let's talk about these.

You might recall how scandalous outsourcing was when it was first put into practice. Major US and UK firms outsourced entire call centers to India. Remember this? All this happened because of basic economics. The west got cheap labour, while jobs were created in the east.

A lot of IT companies have been burnt working with Indian firms. Without going into detail the general consensus is that there is a spin off between quantity and price vs quality.

This is where countries like the Czech Republic come into play. Czechs offer good quality for still competitive pricing.

So this gives you the context of why these Multinationals are here. Often you'll find companies for example HSBC that has whole IT divisions here, even though they don't offer their personal finance products in this country.

I would love to digress and tell you what you can expect once you're hired, but let's keep this article on point.

Brno is nearly as popular as Prague with multinationals because Brno has a massive talent pool. It's the university city where fresh and bright minds are eager to gain first employment experience.

Now you come along! Are you a junior? Are you a senior? How are you going to prove this and what value can you provide?

Before we even go there, I want to be very clear about my next point.

There are a VERY FEW and FINITE number of these multinationals willing to hire you.

Let me say that again - There might be a fair amount of IT work in this country (generally) but the companies that will actually give you an interview and hire you - is MUCH MORE LIMITED.

Ok got that?

So whatever you do, treat the few companies that will hire you as one would treat a precious limited and scarce resource.

Trust me this is something you just cannot afford to be casual with, like you would in the UK or the US or your local home country.

So before we discuss about proving your skills. Let's talk about finding work.

Use agents, and send your CV directly to companies if you are aware of them. ACJobs.cz and Grafton are about the best agencies in the Czech Republic.

Since there are so few companies you can imagine there might be equally as few jobs, however when you go online you actually do see a fair amount of positions that seem to be available, but a lot of this is fudge or if you check the dates - these are old ads.

Regardless don't expect to get an interview soon. I've had one company grant me an interview after nearly 3 months of waiting!

Another thing I want to mention is there is no concept of ASAP in this country. An immediate start typically means 2-3 months. Expect massive delays at every junction.

EG: Send your CV to agent - 1 day turn around time - reply oh hello how are you I can recommend these jobs.... I'll send your CV to them - 1 day delay - Oh fuck it's Friday - Monday (oh fuck I have a headache) - 2 days delay - CV makes it to company - Company 1-2 weeks - gets back to agent - recommends an interview in 1 month! Interview comes wait a week for 2nd interview - wait 3 weeks for a response.

No the above is not fiction but actual experience!

So because of everything mentioned so far you can see why every company is to be treated as a golden opportunity, and why every interview has to count - IN A MASSIVE WAY!

You could be out of work here for months before you even get an interview.

So you say, submit more CV"s , use more agents - well you can't do that if you're already familiar with ALL the companies that are willing to hire you!

If I'm scaring you - Good! But don't worry there is light at the end of this tunnel - I promise you.

So now you need to be prepared. For the most part these multinationals by nature are not that fussy about your exact personality, as long as you are not a total freak or some arrogant douche, you're not going to get massive personality assessments.

Your age may or may not count against you, this too depends not on the company, but on who in any given company is hiring you. It is up to you how you present your best sides. But I have noticed that IT here can be a bit younger (generally) than in the west. Most likely because a lot of people in Czech Republic missed out on the BASIC revolution.

Ok so what it really comes down to is that infernal technical test - and you can be bet your bottom dollar you're getting one!

PREPARE PREPARE PREPARE.

First some more bad news. Every technical test I've had here so far is designed to test computer science fundamentals and idealistic theory.

I'm a .net developer. What I found is that to my astonishment a lot of tests that you find on the net being asked by companies in the US or UK actually are fairly easy, and enjoyable, tests designed to see if you can do your job, not designed to rattle you, as if you climbed in the washing machine.

Technical tests in this country by these multinationals are sadistically hard. But I've been doing some thinking and the reason is obvious, a lot of these tests are templated at picking out University Grads. These tests to a recent comp science grad should be relatively simple, to someone with 15 years of work experience though, these tests probe stuff you very seldom or NEVER work with.

But here is a guideline.

1. OOP - if you aren't a master on OOP you're at risk of not getting hired. Go and read books on the subject. Watch youtube videos, practice coding up stuff - even if you never use it in practice. Get all that fundamental theory down to the point where you could give lectures on it. If you want to ensure you'll get the job, practice giving lectures on it, even if you make some youtube videos, get feedback. Learn to speak about OOP like you believe it.
    
2. Design patterns. Know a few and be ready and willing to speak about them. Again get to the point where you could lecture on this stuff. Simply skimming over the names of the design patterns won't help you much. Like if they question you on the Factory design pattern - don't EVER say "I never use factories!"
    
3. Don't be critical about a technology, unless it has been replaced with a new and better one. For example anonymous types vs tuples.
    
4. Know Garbage collection back to front. Seriously know EVERYTHING you could possibly ever hope to know about Garbage collection because this will always come up!
    
5. Be prepared to write some ridiculous programming task on paper. Sometimes these programming tasks are approachable, but if you have been unemployed for a while and haven't coded, suddenly asked to code something on paper and you could easily hit a blank - NO IDE, No code completion.
    
6. If it's asp.net - page lifecycle and events - back to front no exceptions!
    

Now some more tips.

They want to hear about your experience sure, but in my experience that technical test is 80% of the interview. In your experiences talk with confidence, be professional, use correct terminology and explain how you overcame problems.

So for example. I used to work at this startup which was a load of fun. Actually the work environment was pretty relaxed, but what we produced was awesome, considering such a small team. This was in the UK. We also played a fuck load of unreal tournament.

BUT IN THE INTERVIEW.

We were a small team responsible for the critical core IT operations, ensuring not only the development of sites and applications but also the infrastructure and hosting. Use words like customer demands.

My new strategy now is to have a semi serious sharp crisp tone to my voice when I mention my experiences. Before I took a more casual and fun approach, but this got me nowhere.

Obviously know answers to basic and templated interview questions. Strengths and weaknesses, greatest challenge, where do you see yourself in 5 years.

Lastly don't dick around. Have your interview, be polite not over friendly and then leave.
