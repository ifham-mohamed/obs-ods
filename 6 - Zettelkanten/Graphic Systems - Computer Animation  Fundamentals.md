### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">Design of Animation Sequence</mark>

There are some common steps that are followed in designing and creating computer animations, 
	1. Storyboard Layout
	2. Object Definitions 
	3. Key frame specifications 
	4. Generation of in-between frames

#### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">Storyboard Layout</mark>
  
  - It is the outline of an action. It defines the motion sequences as a set of basic events that are to take place.
  - Depending on the type of animation to be produced, the storyboard could be consisting of a set of rough sketches, or it could be a list of basis ideas for motion.
![[Pasted image 20240801213342.png]]



#### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">Object Definitions</mark>

  - Object definition is specifying the characteristics of an object present in the scene.
  - Specify shape of the object, movement of the object (e.g., Specify bouncing, rolling movements of ball).
  - Has to be described for each object present in the scene.
![[computer-animation-computer-graphics-9-638.webp | 800]]


#### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">Key Frame Specification</mark>

 - Keyframe is a detailed drawing of the scene at a certain time in the animation sequence . 
 - Each object is positioned according to the time for that frame. 
 - In simple animations where lot of detailed motion is not needed, extreme key frames are chosen. 
 - In complex motions, more number of key frames are placed over the timeline for more detailed motion.

![[Pasted image 20240801225317.png | 800]]
#### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">Generation of in-between Frames</mark> 

**Tweening** 
Intermediate frames between **key frames**. 
Creates the illusion of motion. 
Number of in-between frames depend on the media(display). 
In movies or films-> 24FPS 

To record 1 minute of animation film we need, 
24 x 60 = 1440 frames which are aligned in the sequence

### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">8. Straight Ahead Action and Pose-to-Pose Action</mark>

1. Straight Ahead 
	- Animator start from first drawing in the scene and draw all subsequent frames until the end of scene. 
	- This approach tends to yield a more creative and fresher look but can be difficult to time correctly and tweak. 
2. Pose-to-Pose 
	- Pose to Pose is created by drawing or setting up key poses and then drawing or creating in-between images. 
	- This is the basic computer "keyframe" approach to animation. 
	- It is excellent for tweaking timing and planning out the animation ahead of time.

### <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">10. Secondary Action</mark>

Action that results directly from another action

E.g. : Imagine a squirrel, running across your lawn. The movement of the squirrel's legs (primary action) would be animated to express the light, nimble nature of his movement. The agile movement of the squirrel's tail (secondary action) would have a separate and slightly different type of movement than his legs. The squirrel's tail is an example of secondary action.
