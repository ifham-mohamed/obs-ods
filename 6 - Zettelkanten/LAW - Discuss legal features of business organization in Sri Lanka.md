# LAW - Discuss legal features of business organization in Sri Lanka

### Legal Features of Business Organizations in Sri Lanka

In Sri Lanka, business organizations are governed primarily by the Companies Act, No. 07 of 2007, which outlines various types of companies and their legal features. Below is a discussion of the key legal features of business organizations in Sri Lanka:

#### 1. **Legal Personality**

A business organization, particularly a company, is recognized as a separate legal entity from its owners. This means that a company:
- **Can Enter Contracts:** It can enter into contracts and own property in its own name.
- **Can Sue and Be Sued:** It has the ability to initiate legal proceedings and be subject to legal actions.
- **Has Perpetual Succession:** The company continues to exist despite changes in ownership or management.

#### 2. **Types of Companies**

Sri Lankan law recognizes various types of companies, each with distinct legal features:

- **Private Companies:**
  - **Limited by Shares:** Most common; shareholders have limited liability up to the value of their shares.
  - **Limited by Guarantee:** Members agree to contribute a nominal amount in case of insolvency. Common in non-profit organizations.
  - **Unlimited Companies:** Shareholders have unlimited liability for the company’s debts.

- **Public Companies:**
  - **Limited by Shares:** Shares can be offered to the public. Must comply with more rigorous regulations compared to private companies.
  - **Public Limited Company (plc):** Must have its name end with “Public Limited Company” or “plc” and comply with additional regulations for trading on stock exchanges.

#### 3. **Incorporation and Registration**

To become a legal entity, a company must be incorporated through:
- **Submission of Documents:** Including the Memorandum of Association, Articles of Association, Forms No. 1, 18, and 19, and the registration fee.
- **Certification:** The Registrar of Companies issues a Certificate of Incorporation, signifying the company's official formation.

#### 4. **Articles of Association**

These govern the internal operations of the company, including:
- **Meetings and Voting:** Procedures for conducting meetings and decision-making.
- **Shareholder Rights:** Rights related to share ownership, dividends, and transfer.
- **Director Provisions:** Powers, responsibilities, and management structure.

#### 5. **Corporate Governance**

- **Directors and Secretaries:** Companies must have directors and, in some cases, a company secretary. The minimum number of directors varies between public and private companies.
- **Statutory Declarations:** Directors and secretaries must declare compliance with statutory requirements.

#### 6. **Liability**

- **Limited Liability:** Shareholders' liability is limited to the nominal value of their shares. In unlimited companies, shareholders have unlimited liability.
- **Corporate Veil:** The company is a separate entity from its shareholders, but courts can lift the corporate veil in cases of fraud or misconduct.

#### 7. **Financial Requirements**

- **Capital Requirements:** Public companies must meet specific capital requirements and obtain a Trade Certificate before commencing business.
- **Auditing and Reporting:** Companies must maintain proper accounts, undergo audits, and file annual returns with the Registrar.

#### 8. **Regulatory Compliance**

- **Companies Act Compliance:** Companies must adhere to the Companies Act, including filing necessary forms, maintaining registers, and complying with statutory obligations.
- **Tax and Duties:** Companies are liable for income tax and customs duties, and they must comply with regulations regarding tax filings and payments.

#### 9. **Legal Responsibilities and Liabilities**

- **Negligence and Vicarious Liability:** Companies can be held liable for wrongful acts, negligence, or omissions of their employees and agents.
- **Partnerships and Sole Traders:** Unlike companies, partnerships and sole traders do not have separate legal personality and are directly liable for business obligations.

#### 10. **Promoters and Pre-incorporation Contracts**

- **Promoters:** Those who set up the company are responsible for arranging its formation, including drafting documents and securing initial capital. They have fiduciary duties to the company.
- **Pre-incorporation Contracts:** Contracts made before the company is incorporated are not binding on the company but may bind the promoters personally.

Legal Features of Business Organizations in Sri Lanka
1. Separate Legal Entity
A company, upon incorporation, becomes a separate legal person distinct from its shareholders and directors. This allows the company to enter into contracts, own property, sue and be sued in its own name.

2. Limited Liability
Shareholders of a limited liability company are only liable for the company’s debts up to the amount they invested in shares. This means their personal assets are protected from business liabilities.

3. Perpetual Succession
A company has perpetual succession, meaning it continues to exist even if the owners or shareholders change. The company’s existence is not affected by the death, insolvency, or departure of its members.

4. Corporate Personality
The company is recognized as an artificial legal person. This concept allows it to have rights and obligations separate from those of its shareholders.

5. Statutory Compliance
Companies in Sri Lanka must comply with various statutory requirements under the Companies Act, No. 07 of 2007. This includes registration with the Registrar of Companies, filing annual returns, and maintaining proper financial records.

6. Different Types of Companies
There are several types of companies, including private limited companies, public limited companies, companies limited by guarantee, and unlimited companies. Each type has its own legal requirements and characteristics.

7. Governance Structure
Companies have a defined governance structure, typically including a board of directors responsible for overseeing the management of the company. Directors have fiduciary duties to act in the best interest of the company.

8. Transparency and Accountability
Companies are required to maintain transparency through proper accounting practices and disclosures. They must prepare and file annual financial statements and reports, and some may require external audits.

9. Regulatory Oversight
The Registrar of Companies oversees the registration and compliance of companies. Regulatory bodies may also impose specific requirements based on the industry in which the company operates.

Advantages of Establishment of a Company
1. Limited Liability
The primary advantage of a company is limited liability for its shareholders. This encourages investment by reducing the risk to personal assets.

2. Perpetual Succession
A company’s continuous existence allows for long-term planning and stability. It can outlive its founders and continue operations indefinitely.

3. Raising Capital
Companies can raise capital by issuing shares to investors. This makes it easier to fund large projects and expand business operations.

4. Transferability of Shares
Shares in a company can be easily transferred, allowing for flexibility in ownership and the ability to attract and retain investors.

5. Enhanced Credibility
Incorporation often enhances a business’s credibility and image, which can attract customers, partners, and investors.

6. Tax Benefits
Companies may benefit from various tax advantages, such as deductions for business expenses and different tax rates compared to individual taxation.

7. Professional Management
Companies can employ professional managers and directors with specialized skills to run the business, which can improve efficiency and growth prospects.

8. Access to Resources
Incorporation provides access to various resources, including financial markets, government incentives, and business networks.

9. Risk Mitigation
Incorporation helps in mitigating risks through the separation of personal and business liabilities, protecting the personal assets of the owners.

10. Legal Recognition
Being a legally recognized entity, a company can engage in various commercial activities and enter into binding contracts, thereby expanding its business opportunities.
### Conclusion

The legal framework for business organizations in Sri Lanka, primarily governed by the Companies Act, provides a structured approach to forming and operating companies. It ensures that companies are recognized as distinct legal entities with their own rights and responsibilities, while also setting out clear guidelines for their governance, financial operations, and regulatory compliance.
