# LAW - Legal Systems of Sri Lanka 

*In the earlier two lessons we discussed different classification of law and sources of the Law in Sri Lanka. Even through we already learnt how law ensures proper functioning of a society. This lesson will enable to you to gain basic understanding of the legal systems of Sri Lanka such as Roman- Dutch Law , English Law , Kandyan Law, Thesawalamai Law and Muslim Law .*

### Roman-Dutch Law in Sri Lanka

**Background:**

- **Introduced by the Dutch:** The Dutch brought Roman-Dutch Law to Sri Lanka and used it from 1656 to 1796.
- **Continued by the British:** When the British took over in 1796, they kept the existing laws, including Roman-Dutch Law.

**Common Law:**
- **Applies to Everyone:** Roman-Dutch Law is the “common law” of Sri Lanka, meaning it generally applies to everyone unless they follow a special personal law (like Kandyan law, Thesawalamai, or Muslim Law).

**Residual Law:**
- **Fallback Law:** If another law (like English law) doesn’t cover a specific issue, Roman-Dutch Law is used to resolve it.

**Sources of Roman-Dutch Law:**
1. **Treatises of Jurists:** Books written by Dutch legal experts, like Hugo Grotius, who is considered the father of Roman-Dutch Law.
2. **Statutes:** Laws enacted by the Dutch, especially from their headquarters in Batavia (now Jakarta).
3. **Judicial Decisions:** Court decisions from the Netherlands and Sri Lanka, as well as South African cases, which are used as references.

**Modern Use:**

- **Limited to Few Countries:** Today, Roman-Dutch Law is mainly used in Sri Lanka, South Africa, and Zimbabwe.
#### Applications of Roman-Dutch Law in Sri Lanka

**Dutch and British Influence:**

- **Dutch Period:** The Dutch applied their law to both Europeans and locals, especially when local laws were silent or didn’t match their views on morality or public policy.
- **British Period:** During British rule, many parts of Roman-Dutch Law, like Evidence, Criminal, Constitutional, and Commercial Law, were replaced. Now, Roman-Dutch Law mainly applies to Private Law areas like Property, Delict (similar to tort law), and Contract Law.

**Restricted Application:**

- **British Legislation:** British laws limited the use of Roman-Dutch Law. For example, cheques and promissory notes are governed by English-based laws like the Bills of Exchange Ordinance (1927) and the Sale of Goods Ordinance (1896).
- **Affected Areas:** Major areas like Commercial Law, Criminal Law, Contract Law, Evidence Law, and Public Law are now mostly governed by English law. English judges often prefer English legal solutions.

**Court Decisions:**

- **Korossa Rubber Company vs. Silva:** It was stated that pure Roman-Dutch Law doesn’t exist entirely in Sri Lanka due to statutes and judicial decisions.
- **Haniffa vs. Ocean Accident Insurance Corporation:** The Supreme Court highlighted a difference between Roman-Dutch Law and English Law regarding contract performance. In Roman-Dutch Law, the creditor must seek out the debtor, while in English Law, the debtor must seek out the creditor.


### English Law

**Sources of English Law:**

1. **Common Law:** Developed through the writings of English jurists (like Blackstone) and court decisions. The Queen is considered the source of common law. Courts follow the principle of **Stare Decisis** (using past decisions as precedents).
2. **Equity:** Originated from the Chancery Courts in England, focusing on fairness and natural justice. It complements common law and is considered universal.
3. **Legislation:** Laws passed by the Parliament.

#### Reception of English Law in Sri Lanka

**Two Methods of Incorporation:**

1. **By Statute:**
    
    - **Copying UK Laws:** Some UK laws were copied and enacted in Sri Lanka, like the Companies Ordinance of 1938 and the Sale of Goods Ordinance of 1896.
    - **Adopting Principles:** Principles from English court decisions were codified into local laws, like the Penal Code of 1883 and the Evidence Ordinance of 1895.
    - **Filling Gaps:** English law is used when local laws are silent on an issue, as seen in Section 100 of the Evidence Ordinance of 1895.
    - **Extending UK Acts:** Certain UK Acts were extended to Sri Lanka during the British period, like the Air Navigation Act.
    - **British Sovereignty:** English law was applied as a result of British rule over Sri Lanka.
2. **By Judicial Activism:**
    
    - **Adopting English Concepts:** In the 19th century, English legal concepts were sometimes adopted by judges without specific reasons. Early British judges, unfamiliar with Roman-Dutch Law, often turned to English law.
    - **Reasons for Adoption:**
        - Roman-Dutch Law didn’t cover certain situations (e.g., De Silva vs. Cassim).
        - Roman-Dutch Law was similar to English law (e.g., Boid vs. Staples).

#### Applicability of English Law in Sri Lanka

**Influence on Business and Commerce:**

- **British Introduction:** After the British took over from the Dutch in 1796, they introduced English law, which now governs many business and commercial activities in Sri Lanka.

**Areas Governed by English Law:**

- **Criminal Law, Mercantile Law, Law of Evidence, Law of Agency, Banking Law, Companies Law:** These areas are primarily governed by English principles.
- **Law of Persons, Law of Property, Contract Law, Law of Torts (Delict):** These are mainly based on Roman-Dutch Law but have been modified by legislation and English judicial decisions.

**Judicial Decisions:**

- **English Principles in Contracts:** English principles have been applied in contract law through judicial decisions. For example, in cases like Perera vs. Thisera and Udalagama vs. Banda, the English law of undue influence was recognized as part of Sri Lankan law.

**Constitutional Influence:**

- **First Constitution:** The first Constitution of independent Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) was modeled after England’s, and the 1978 Constitution still reflects some English principles.

**Court System and Practices:**

- **Influence on Courts:** The structure, practices, and traditions of Sri Lankan courts, as well as the legal profession, have been influenced by English ideas.

#### Personal Laws in Sri Lanka

**What Are Personal Laws?**

- **Specific to Communities:** Kandyan Law, Thesawalamai Law, and Muslim Law are legal systems that apply only to specific communities in Sri Lanka.
- **Not General:** These laws are not applied to everyone in the country, only to those who belong to the respective communities.

**Difference from Territorial Law:**

- **Personal Law:** Applies to specific groups of people based on their community or religion.
- **Territorial Law:** Applies to everyone living in a particular area or territory, regardless of their community or religion.

**Examples of Personal Laws:**

1. **Kandyan Law:** Applies to the Kandyan Sinhalese community.
2. **Thesawalamai Law:** Applies to the Tamil community in the Jaffna Peninsula.
3. **Muslim Law:** Applies to the Muslim community.

These personal laws have unique features and rules that cater to the traditions and customs of their respective communities.


### Kandyan Law

**What is Kandyan Law?**

- **Customary Law:** Kandyan Law is based on the traditional customs of the Sinhalese people. Today, it applies specifically to the Kandyan Sinhalese community.

#### Sources of Kandyan Law

1. **Written Decrees:**
    - **Sannasas:** Royal grants written on copper plates.
    - **Talipots:** Deeds written on palm leaves.
    - **Sittu:** Written decrees given to the successful party in land matters.
2. **Books of Authority:**
    - **Sir John D’oyly’s Works:** “A Sketch of the Constitution of the Kandyan Kingdom” and “Neethi Niganduwa” (edited by T.B. Panabokke and Turner).
    - **Simon Sower’s “The Digest”.**
    - **John Armour’s Notes on Kandyan Law.**
3. **Decisions of the Board of Commissioners:**
    - From 1815 to 1833, the Kandyan provinces were administered by the Board of Commissioners, who consulted local chiefs. Their minutes and decisions from 1816 to 1833 are important sources.
4. **Case Law:**
    - **Stare Decisis:** Sri Lankan courts follow the principle of using past judicial decisions as precedents. Once a principle of Kandyan Law is established in a court decision, it becomes binding. Examples include cases like Kershow vs. Nicoll and Williams vs. Robertson.
5. **Statute Law (Legislation):**
    - **Modern Legislation:** Some laws have modified ancient Kandyan Law principles. Important statutes include:
        - **Declaration and Amendment of Kandyan Law Ordinance No: 39 of 1938.**
        - **Kandyan Marriage and Divorce Act No: 44 of 1952.**
        - **Kandyan Succession Ordinance of 1917.**
#### Application of Kandyan Law

**Historical Context:**
- **Territorial Application:** Originally, Kandyan Law applied to everyone living in the Kandyan provinces during the times of the Sinhalese kings.
- **Personal Law Today:** Now, Kandyan Law applies only to the Kandyan Sinhalese community, as decided in the case of Williams vs. Robertson.

#### Key Cases

1. **Kershow vs. Nicoll:**
    - **Initial Ruling:** The Supreme Court initially ruled that Kandyan Law should apply to all cases in the Kandyan provinces, even to a European married woman living there.
    - **Overruled:** This decision was later overruled by the Supreme Court in Williams vs. Robertson, which stated that Kandyan Law applies only to Kandyan Sinhalese.
2. **Williams vs. Robertson:**
    - **Final Decision:** Established that Kandyan Law is a personal law for Kandyan Sinhalese only.
3. **Wijesinghe vs. Wijesinghe:**
    - **Succession Law:** A low country Sinhalese living in the Kandyan provinces was ruled to be governed by Roman-Dutch Law for matters of succession.

#### Modern Application

**Marriage and Property:**
- **Marriage Types:** Kandyan Law distinguishes between two types of marriages:
    - **Binna Marriage:** The husband lives with the wife’s family.
    - **Diga Marriage:** The wife moves to live with the husband’s family or in a separate home.
- **Registration:** Marriages must be registered under the Kandyan Marriage and Divorce Act No: 44 of 1952.
- **Property Types:**
    - **Inherited Property (Paraveni):** Property passed down through generations.
    - **Acquired Property (Lathhimiya):** Property acquired during one’s lifetime.

**Geographical Applicability:**
- **Regions Covered:** Kandyan Law applies in specific regions, including:
    - Central Province
    - Sabaragamuwa Province
    - Uva Province
    - North Central Province
    - Kurunegala District and parts of Puttalam District in the North Western Province
    - Some areas of the Eastern Province

### Thesawalamai Law

**What is Thesawalamai Law?**
- **Customary Law:** Thesawalamai Law is the traditional law for the Tamil people living in Jaffna. The word “Thesawalamai” means “customs of the land.”
- **Influences:** It has been influenced by Hindu law and Roman-Dutch law.

#### Sources of Thesawalamai Law

1. **Thesawalamai Code (1707):** A written collection of laws.
2. **Thesawalamai Regulations (1806):** Additional rules and regulations.
3. **Court Decisions:** Judicial decisions that incorporate Thesawalamai Law.
4. **Statute Law:** Legislation like the Jaffna Matrimonial Rights and Inheritance Ordinance of 1911 and the Thesawalamai Pre-emption Ordinance of 1947.
5. **Customary Laws:** Traditions and customs of the Tamil people.

#### Applicability of Thesawalamai Law

**Personal and Territorial Law:**
- **Personal Law:** Applies to Tamil people (Malabar inhabitants) of Jaffna.
- **Territorial Law:** Applies to property matters in Jaffna, like mortgages and servitudes.

**Key Features:**
- **Pre-emption Right:** If someone wants to sell their property, they must first offer it to their genuine neighbor.
- **Women’s Property Rights:** Women cannot sell immovable property without their husband’s consent.
- **Servitudes:** Recognizes certain rights, like gathering fruits from branches that overhang a neighbor’s land. The neighbor has no right to these fruits.


### Muslim Law

**What is Muslim Law?**

- **Personal Law:** Muslim Law is based on the religion of Islam and applies to all followers of Islam in Sri Lanka.
- **Origins:** It originated from pre-Islamic Arabian customs and is influenced by Islamic teachings.

#### Key Concepts

- **Shariat:** The canon law of Islam, guiding ethical duties.
- **Fiqh:** Rules that citizens must follow.

#### Sources of Muslim Law

1. **Quran:** The holy book of Islam, containing the teachings of the Prophet.
2. **Hadiths:** Oral teachings and actions of the Prophet.
3. **Ijma:** Consensus of Muslim scholars on legal issues.
4. **Qiyas:** Analogical reasoning based on the Quran and Hadiths.
5. **Legislation:** Laws like the Mohammedan Code of 1806, Muslim Marriage and Divorce Act of 1951, and Wakfs Act of 1956.
6. **Customs:** Traditional practices like “Cheeddanam” and “Kaikooly” (dowry property).
7. **Case Law:** Judicial decisions that shape the application of Muslim Law.

#### Applicability in Sri Lanka

- **Personal Law:** Applies to Muslims in matters like marriage, divorce, and inheritance.
- **Marriage:** A contract (Nikah) requiring offer and acceptance, with the Wali (guardian) seeking the bride’s consent.
- **Divorce:** After divorce, a woman must wait a period (iddat) before remarrying to ensure paternity clarity.
- **Dowry:** Special rules for dowry payments like Mahar, Kaikooli, and Cheedanum. Kaikooli is a gift from the bride’s father, recoverable upon divorce.
- **Polygamy:** A Muslim man can have up to four wives.
- **Divorce Systems:** Men can divorce through Talaq, while women can use Khul or Fasah systems.
- **Quasi Courts:** Handle divorce applications under Muslim Law.

#### Sects

- **Sunni and Shia:** The two main sects of Islam. Sri Lankan Muslims are mainly Shafii, a sub-sect of Sunni.
