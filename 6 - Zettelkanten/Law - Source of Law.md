# Law - Source of Law

## 1. Legislation (Enacted Law, Statute Law)

**Legislation** refers to laws made by the Parliament. These laws are called Acts or Ordinances. Here’s a simple breakdown:

- **Acts and Ordinances**: These are laws passed by Parliament. They are the main sources of law today and are becoming more important as governments take on more roles in society.
- **Parliament’s Role**: Parliament is the branch of government that makes laws. It has the power to create laws, but it must follow the constitution.

### Types of Local Legislation

1. **Regulations and Proclamations (1801-1833)**: Early laws issued by the Governor. Examples include:
    - Tax regulations for roads and bridges (1831)
    - Export duty on tobacco (1804)
2. **Ordinances (1833-1947)**: Laws passed by colonial legislatures. Examples include:
    - Land Settlement Ordinance (1931)
    - Forests Ordinance (1907)
3. **Acts (1948-1972)**: Laws passed by Parliament after independence. Examples include:
    - Fertilizers Act (1961)
    - Motor Traffic Act (1951)
    - Land Acquisition Act (1950)
4. **Laws under the 1972 Republican Constitution**: Laws passed by the National State Assembly. Examples include:
    - Agricultural Insurance Law (1973)
    - Janawasa Law (1976)
5. **Acts under the 1978 Republican Constitution**: More recent laws passed by Parliament. Examples include:
    - Fisheries & Aquatic Resources Act (1996)
    - Electricity Reform Act (2002)
    - Intellectual Property Act (2003)
    - Computer Crimes Act (2007)
    - Electronic Transactions Act (2006)
    - Civil Aviation Authority Act (2002)
    - Information Communication Technology Act (2003)

## 2. Judicial Precedent (Stare Decisis)

- **Judicial Precedent**: This means that judges make decisions based on previous court rulings. It’s also known as “case law” or “judges-made law.”
- **Stare Decisis**: This is a Latin term meaning “to stand by things decided.” It means that courts follow the rulings of previous cases to ensure consistency in the law.

### How It Works in Sri Lanka

- **Adopted from England**: Sri Lanka adopted this system from the British during their colonial period.
- **Superior Courts**: The highest courts in Sri Lanka are the Supreme Court and the Court of Appeal. These courts make final decisions on legal matters.
- **Making New Law**: If there’s a new issue not covered by existing laws, a ruling by these superior courts becomes the law for future cases.
- **Following Legislation**: If there is already a law on the issue, judges must follow it. They can interpret unclear laws but cannot ignore clear ones.


## 3. Equity
### Equity in Simple Terms

**Equity** refers to fairness and justice in the legal system. Here’s a simplified explanation:

- **Natural Justice**: Equity means ensuring fairness and justice in legal matters.
- **Part of English Law**: It originated in England and is a part of their legal system.

### Development and Application

- **Distinct from Common Law**: Equity developed separately from common law in church courts in England.
- **Universal Application**: The principles of equity are considered to be applicable everywhere.

### Key Principles of Equity

1. **Just and Reasonable Interpretation**: Equity ensures that laws are interpreted in a fair and reasonable manner.
2. **Filling Gaps**: When there are gaps or unclear areas in the legal system, equity can be used to fill those gaps.

### Important Maxims (Principles) of Equity

1. **Equity Follows the Law**: Equity works alongside the law and does not override it.
2. **Clean Hands Doctrine**: To seek fairness in court, a person must act fairly themselves and not be involved in wrongdoing.

### Abstract Nature

- **Abstract Concept**: Unlike other sources of law, equity is more about the principles behind the law rather than specific rules.

## 4. Religion
### Religion as a Source of Law

**Religion** plays a role in shaping laws through both legislation and customs. Here’s a simplified explanation:

- **Historical Influence**: In ancient societies, many rules and customs were based on religious teachings and moral principles. For example, Hindu and Muslim laws have influenced the legal systems in many Asian countries.
    
- **Modern Application**: Although the direct influence of religious laws has decreased, they still play a role in some countries.
    

### Religion in Sri Lanka

- **Main Religions**: The primary religions in Sri Lanka are Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, and Islam.
- **Legal Influence**: These religions have influenced the country’s laws, especially Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism.
- **Court Administration**: Sri Lankan courts sometimes apply legal rules derived from these religions.


## 5. Customs
### Customs as a Source of Law

**Customs** are traditional practices and rules that have become accepted as law over time. 

Here’s a simplified explanation:

- **Recognition by Courts**: For a custom to be recognized as law by courts, it must meet certain criteria:
- **Ancient and Well-Established**: The custom must have been practiced for a long time.
- **Reasonable**: The custom must be fair and sensible.
- **Certain**: The custom must be clear and specific.
- **Uniformly Used**: The custom must be consistently followed by the community.

### Legal Customs

- **Authority of Courts**: Courts have the power to recognize and enforce certain customs as legal customs. These legal customs are binding and have consistent enforcement.

**Examples**:
- **Fishing Rights**: The right of any member of the public to fish in the open sea is a recognized custom in Sri Lanka.
- **Customary Marriages**: Courts in Sri Lanka have recognized customary marriages as valid.

### Definition by Jurists

- **Unwritten Right**: Jurists define custom as an unwritten right that has developed gradually through practice and has the force of law.

In summary, customs are traditional practices that can become legally binding if they are ancient, reasonable, certain, and uniformly used. Courts have the authority to recognize and enforce these customs as law.
