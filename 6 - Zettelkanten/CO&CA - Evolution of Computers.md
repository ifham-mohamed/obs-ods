# CO&CA - Evolution of Computers

## 1. Pre-Mechanical Era (3000 BCE - 1450 CE)

During the pre-mechanical era, which spanned from 3000 BCE to 1450 CE, early civilizations developed various calculation devices and mechanical tools to aid in their daily activities. Some key developments include:

- [The invention of the abacus by the Chinese around 2600 BCE](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
- [The development of the Antikythera mechanism, an ancient Greek analog computer used to predict astronomical positions and eclipses, around 100 BCE](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)

## 2. Mechanical Era (1450 - 1840)

The mechanical era saw the development of increasingly sophisticated mechanical calculators and analytical engines. Key events include:

- [In 1623, Wilhelm Schickard designed the first mechanical calculator](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
- [In 1642, Blaise Pascal invented the Pascaline, one of the first mechanical calculators](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
- [In 1671, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz invented the stepped gear calculator and helped develop the binary number system](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
- [In 1801, Joseph Marie Jacquard used punch cards to control textile looms, an early example of programmable logic](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
- [In 1822, Charles Babbage proposed the Analytical Engine, an early general-purpose computer design that used punch cards for input](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)

## 3. Electro-Mechanical Era (1840 - 1940)

The electro-mechanical era combined mechanical and electrical technologies to create more advanced calculators and early computers. Key developments include:

- In 1936, Alan Turing introduced the concept of a universal machine, laying the foundations for modern computing[](https://www.g2.com/articles/history-of-computers)
- In 1937, John Atanasoff designed the first electronic digital computer at Iowa State University[](https://www.g2.com/articles/history-of-computers)
- In 1943, the British developed the Colossus, one of the first programmable, electronic digital computers, to help break German codes during WWII[](https://pandorafms.com/blog/computer-history/)
- In 1946, ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer), the first general-purpose electronic computer, was completed at the University of Pennsylvania[](https://www.g2.com/articles/history-of-computers)

## 4. The First Generation: Vacuum Tubes (1940s-1950s)

The first generation of computers used vacuum tubes for digital logic elements and memory. Key characteristics include:

- Vacuum tubes were used for digital logic and memory
- Early computers were large, expensive, and power-hungry
- Programming was done using plug boards and switches
- Notable examples include the UNIVAC I and IBM 701[](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)

## 5. The Second Generation: Transistors (1950s-1960s)

The second generation of computers replaced vacuum tubes with transistors, leading to smaller, cheaper, and more reliable machines. Key developments include:

- Transistors replaced vacuum tubes, enabling smaller and more reliable computers
- High-level programming languages like FORTRAN and COBOL were developed
- Magnetic core memory was introduced for data storage
- Notable examples include the IBM 1401 and DEC PDP-1[](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)

## 6. The Third Generation: Integrated Circuits (1960s-1970s)

The third generation saw the introduction of integrated circuits, which packed multiple transistors onto a single chip. This led to further reductions in size, cost, and power consumption. Key characteristics include:

- Integrated circuits packed multiple transistors onto a single chip
- Magnetic core memory was replaced by semiconductor memory
- Operating systems provided a more user-friendly interface
- Notable examples include the IBM System/360 and DEC PDP-8[](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)

## 7. Beyond the Third Generation

Later generations saw continued miniaturization and the rise of microprocessors, personal computers, and the internet. Key developments include:

- Large-scale integration (LSI) packed more than 1,000 components per chip
- Very-large-scale integration (VLSI) achieved over 10,000 components per chip
- The microprocessor enabled the development of personal computers
- [The internet and World Wide Web revolutionized communication and information sharing](https://study.com/academy/lesson/history-of-computers-timeline-evolution.html)
