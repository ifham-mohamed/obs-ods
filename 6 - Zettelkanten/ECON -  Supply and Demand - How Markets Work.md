# ECON -  Supply and Demand - How Markets Work

### Market Forces of Supply and Demand

#### Key Concepts

- **Supply and Demand as Forces**: These are called forces because they influence prices by acting in different ways. Supply and demand pressures cause prices to change.
- **Behavior and Interaction**: The theory examines how sellers and buyers behave and interact, influencing market outcomes.
- **Price Signals**: Prices act as signals that influence the behavior of both buyers and sellers, helping to allocate scarce resources efficiently.
- **Price vs. Cost**:
    - **Price**: The amount of money a buyer must pay to acquire something.
    - **Cost**: The payment made to factor inputs in production.

### Assumptions of the Model of Supply and Demand

- **Purpose of Models**: Economists use models to represent reality, though no model is perfect. The supply and demand model is based on several assumptions and is known as the perfectly competitive market model.
- **Assumptions**:
    - **Many Buyers and Sellers**: There are numerous participants in the market.
    - **Homogeneous Goods**: The goods offered by different sellers are identical.
    - **Perfect Information**: All buyers and sellers have complete information about the market.
    - **Freedom of Entry and Exit**: Firms can freely enter or exit the market.
    - **Clearly Defined Buyers and Sellers**: The roles of buyers and sellers are distinct.
    - **Clearly Defined Property Rights**: Ownership rights are well-defined and enforceable.
    - **Zero Transaction Costs**: There are no costs associated with making transactions.
    - **Rational Behavior**: Buyers and sellers act rationally to maximize their utility and profit.

### Criticisms and Real-World Application

- **Unrealistic Assumptions**: Critics argue that the model’s assumptions are too unrealistic, limiting its predictive power.
- **Mindfulness of Assumptions**: It’s important to be aware of these assumptions when using the model.
- **Contextual Insights**: By examining different contexts, we can gain insights and understand behaviors and outcomes that might differ from the model’s predictions.
- **Practical Use**: Despite its unrealistic assumptions, the supply and demand model allows us to make some predictions and provides an approximation to many everyday experiences. It helps assess how markets might behave differently when these assumptions are relaxed.

### Demand

**Definition**:

- **Demand** is the willingness and ability to buy a good or service.

**Key Points**:

- **Willingness**: This refers to the desire or interest to purchase a product.
- **Ability**: This refers to having the financial means or resources to make the purchase.

**Important Note**:

- **Both willingness and ability are necessary** for demand to exist. If a person is willing to buy something but lacks the financial means, or if they have the financial means but no desire to buy, demand will not be created.

### Law of Demand

**Definition**:

- The **Law of Demand** states that the quantity demanded of a good decreases when the price of that good increases, **cēterīs paribus** (all other things being equal).

**Key Points**:

- **Inverse Relationship**: There is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded. As the price rises, the quantity demanded falls, and vice versa.
- **Cēterīs Paribus**: This Latin phrase means “all other things being equal.” It indicates that the law holds true only when other factors affecting demand remain constant.

### Substitution Effect and Income Effect

#### Substitution Effect

- **Definition**: The substitution effect occurs when consumers replace one product with another due to a change in relative prices.
- **Example**: If the price of coffee increases, consumers might buy more tea instead, assuming tea is relatively cheaper. This effect can work both ways, with consumers switching to more expensive items if their prices drop relative to others.

#### Income Effect

- **Definition**: The income effect refers to the change in consumption resulting from a change in real income.
- **Impact of Price Changes**:
    - **Price Increase**: When the price of a commodity rises, the real income of consumers effectively decreases, meaning they can buy less with the same amount of money. This leads to a reduction in the quantity demanded.
    - **Price Decrease**: Conversely, when the price of a commodity falls, the real income of consumers increases, allowing them to buy more with the same amount of money. This leads to an increase in the quantity demanded.


### Why the Demand Curve is Downward Sloping

The demand curve slopes downward from left to right, indicating that as the price of a good decreases, the quantity demanded increases, and vice versa. This inverse relationship is due to several factors:

1. **Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility**:
    - As consumers consume more units of a good, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) from each additional unit decreases. Therefore, consumers are willing to pay less for additional units, leading to a downward-sloping demand curve.
2. **Substitution Effect**:
    - When the price of a good rises, consumers may substitute it with a cheaper alternative, reducing the quantity demanded of the more expensive good. Conversely, if the price falls, consumers may switch from substitutes to the cheaper good, increasing its demand.
3. **Income Effect**:
    - A price increase effectively reduces consumers’ real income, leading them to buy less of the good. Conversely, a price decrease increases real income, allowing consumers to buy more.

### Shifts in the Demand Curve

![[Pasted image 20240820130423.png | 700]]

- **Shift to the Right**: Indicates an increase in demand at the same price level.
- **Shift to the Left**: Indicates a decrease in demand at the same price level.

#### Factors Causing Shifts:

1. **Income**:
    
    - **Normal Goods**: An increase in income leads to an increase in demand.
    - **Inferior Goods**: An increase in income leads to a decrease in demand.
2. **Prices of Related Goods**:
    
    - **Substitutes**: An increase in the price of one good leads to an increase in demand for its substitute.
    - **Complements**: An increase in the price of one good leads to a decrease in demand for its complement.
3. **Tastes**: Changes in consumer preferences can shift demand.
    
4. **Expectations**: Expectations about future prices or income can affect current demand.
    
5. **Size and Structure of Population**: Changes in population size or demographics can influence demand.
    

These concepts help explain the behavior of consumers in response to price changes and other factors affecting demand. If you have any more questions or need further clarification, feel free to ask!

- **Expectations**: If consumers expect future prices to rise, current demand may increase, shifting the demand curve to the right.
- **Size and Structure of Population**: Changes in the population size or its demographic structure can shift the demand curve. For example, an increase in population size generally shifts the demand curve to the right.

### Supply

**Definition**:

- **Supply**: The quantity of any good or service that sellers are willing and able to sell at different market prices.

### Law of Supply

**Definition**:

- The **Law of Supply** states that the quantity supplied of a good rises when the price of the good rises, **cēterīs paribus** (all other things being equal).

**Key Points**:

- **Positive Relationship**: There is a positive relationship between price and quantity supplied. As the price increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa.

- **Shift to the Left**: Indicates a decrease in supply at the same price level.

#### Factors Causing Shifts:

1. **Input Prices**: Changes in the prices of factors of production can shift the supply curve. An increase in input prices shifts the supply curve to the left.
2. **Technology**: Technological advancements can increase supply, shifting the supply curve to the right.
3. **Expectations**: Expectations about future prices can affect current supply. If producers expect higher future prices, they may reduce current supply, shifting the curve to the left.
4. **Number of Sellers**: An increase in the number of sellers in the market increases supply, shifting the supply curve to the right.
5. **Natural/Social Factors**: Factors such as weather conditions, natural disasters, and social expectations can shift the supply curve.
