### Running Glossary (live; will grow as we teach)

- **International Business** — Cross-border commercial transactions (sales, investments, transportation) among countries; by private firms (profit) and governments (profit or other reasons). (p. 3 & 8, “Definition”; “Globalization”)

- **Globalization** — Growing interdependence of economies, cultures, and populations via cross-border trade, tech, investment, people, information flows. (p. 8, “Globalization”)

- **Trade (% of GDP)** — Sum of exports + imports (goods & services) as a share of GDP. (p. 9, “Trade (WDI context)”)

- **Trade surplus / deficit** — Sells more than buys / buys more than sells. (p. 11, “Trade Goes International”)

- **Modes** — Export/Import; Licensing/Franchising; Joint Ventures/Alliances; FDI. (p. 11)

- **FDI** — Investment in a foreign entity; often with controlling interest; for materials, global presence, expansion. (p. 13)

- **Tariff** — Tax on imports that raises foreign-goods prices; used for protection. (p. 15)

- **Strategic Alliance** — Voluntary, resource/knowledge-sharing arrangement to develop processes/products/services; motives and types on p. 16–17. (p. 16–17)

- **MNE / MNC** — Types of international organizations (p. 18).

# STEP 2 — Teach by Modules (Interactive Loop)

## Module 1 — Foundations of International Business (p. 3–6)

### 1) Simple first (plain language)

- **What is international business?**  
    Doing business **across country borders**—selling goods/services, investing money, and moving products between countries. (p. 3, “Definition”; p. 8 expands transactions)

- **Why firms go international?**

    1. **Sell more** (new customers), 2) **Get resources** (materials, skills, tech), 3) **Reduce risk** (don’t depend on one country). (p. 5, “Why Companies Engage…”)

- **Why it’s important?**  
    It drives **globalization**, encourages **diversification**, builds **cultural awareness**, supports **political/economic stability**, spreads **innovation/tech**, improves **supply chains**, and grows **career opportunities**. (p. 6, “Importance of International Business”)


### 2) A bit deeper (formal nuance)

- The slide set frames international business as **commercial transactions** (sales, investments, transport) **among countries**, with **private** firms typically **profit-oriented**, and **governments** participating **for profit or other reasons** (e.g., policy, development). (p. 8, “Globalization” — transaction bullets)

- “**Factors in International Business Operations**” hints at the **multi-variable** environment (costs, culture, politics, tech) you must scan before entering a market. (p. 4, “Factors …”)

### Worked example (from slides’ logic)

- **Scenario**: A Sri Lankan startup selling eco-bottles.
    - **Sales expansion**: targets Maldives and UAE tourists. (p. 5)
    - **Resource acquisition**: imports specialized biodegradable resin unavailable locally. (p. 5)
    - **Risk reduction**: if local demand dips, export markets stabilize revenue. (p. 5)
    - **Importance links**: gains supply-chain partners, learns packaging tech, hires cross-cultural staff. (p. 6)

### Quick analogy

- **Analogy: “Neighborhood market → City mall → Global bazaar.”**  
    Moving from selling on your street to a city mall is like expanding domestically; moving to the **global bazaar** (other countries) is international business—**more customers**, **more suppliers**, and **more rules**. (p. 5–6)
