# TAX

### Explanation of the Text

**Tax Definition**:

- **Compulsory Charge**: Tax is a mandatory payment that individuals and businesses must make to the government or a government-authorized institution.
- **No Direct Benefit**: When you pay taxes, you don’t receive a direct, immediate benefit in return, like you would when you buy a product or service.

The Government collects revenue in the form of taxes, charges, levies, fines, etc. 

Taxes are imposed in two forms as direct and indirect taxes. Direct tax is levied directly on personal & corporate income and profits. Whereas indirect tax is levied on the price of a good or services


**Functions and principles of taxation**
1. Primary Function
2. Economic Function 
3. Social Function

#### Primary function 
Raising funds for public expenditure.

Examples for public expenditures: 
- free medical services
- infrastructure facilities
- security & law
- free education service
- road development
- electricity and water

#### Economic functions of taxation: 
- Set local and foreign investment direction - The State could encourage or discourage private sector investments by using taxation tools such as rates and incentives. 
- Protect local industries from foreign competition– Vital local industries could be protected from foreign competition via tools such as import taxes. 

#### Social functions of taxation: 
- Discourage activities which are undesirable by the society- Tax tools could be used to curb undesirable activities such as consumption of liquor & tobacco or gaming & gambling. 
- Encourage activities which are desirable by the society- Desirable activities such as savings, retirement investments, innovation, research & development, etc. could be encouraged via taxation system. 
- Reduce income disparity between rich and poor– Income inequality in the society could be reduced via taxation system using progressive tax rates, rebates, etc.

### Principles of Taxation

The principles of taxation are guidelines that help governments design and implement effective tax systems. Here’s a brief explanation of each principle:

1. **Equity**
    - **Horizontal Equity**: Taxpayers with similar financial situations should pay similar amounts in taxes.
    - **Vertical Equity**: Taxpayers with greater ability to pay (higher income) should pay more in taxes.
2. **Certainty**
    - Tax laws should be clear and certain, so taxpayers know when, how, and how much tax they need to pay.
3. **Convenience and Voluntary Compliance**
    - The tax system should be convenient for taxpayers to comply with, encouraging voluntary compliance.
4. **Economy/Efficiency**
    - The cost of collecting taxes should be kept to a minimum for both the government and taxpayers.
5. **Progressivity**
    - The tax rate should increase as the taxpayer’s ability to pay increases, ensuring that those with higher incomes pay a higher percentage of their income in taxes.
6. **Adequacy**
    - The tax system should generate sufficient revenue to meet the government’s expenditure needs.
7. **Broad Base**
    - Taxes should be spread across a wide range of income sources and economic activities to avoid over-reliance on a single source.
8. **Simplicity**
    - The tax system should be simple to understand and administer, reducing the likelihood of errors and evasion.


### Classification of Taxes

Taxes can be classified into two main categories: **Direct Taxes** and **Indirect Taxes**.

#### Direct Taxes

- **Definition**: These are taxes that are paid directly by the individual or organization to the government. The tax burden cannot be shifted to another party.
- **Example**: **Income Tax** - Individuals and businesses pay this tax directly on their earnings or profits.

#### Indirect Taxes

- **Definition**: These are taxes that are collected by an intermediary (such as a retailer) from the person who ultimately bears the economic burden of the tax (such as the consumer). The tax burden can be shifted from one party to another.
- **Example**: **Value Added Tax (VAT)** - This tax is collected by businesses at each stage of production and distribution, but the final burden falls on the consumer who purchases the goods or services.
#### Indirect Benefits of Taxation

Even though paying taxes doesn’t provide a direct benefit, it offers several **indirect benefits** to the public at large:

1. **Public Services**:
    - Taxes fund essential public services such as healthcare, education, and infrastructure (roads, bridges, public transportation).
    - These services improve the quality of life for everyone in the community.
2. **Economic Stability**:
    - Taxes help maintain economic stability by funding government operations and public projects.
    - This can lead to job creation and economic growth.
3. **Social Welfare**:
    - Taxes support social welfare programs that assist the less fortunate, reducing poverty and inequality.
    - Programs like unemployment benefits, social security, and public housing are funded by taxes.
4. **Security and Law Enforcement**:
    - Taxes fund the police, military, and other security services that protect citizens and maintain order.
    - This ensures a safe and stable environment for everyone.
5. **Infrastructure Development**:
    - Taxes are used to build and maintain infrastructure such as roads, bridges, and public transportation systems.
    - Good infrastructure facilitates trade, travel, and overall economic development.
6. **Environmental Protection**:
    - Taxes can be used to fund environmental protection initiatives, ensuring a cleaner and healthier environment for future generations.

### Direct and Indirect Taxes - Exercise

Here’s a breakdown of the examples provided for direct and indirect taxes:

#### Direct Taxes

- **Income Tax**: This is a tax levied directly on personal and corporate income. Individuals and businesses pay this tax directly to the government based on their earnings or profits.

#### Indirect Taxes

- **Value Added Tax (VAT)**: This is a tax on the value added to goods and services at each stage of production or distribution. It is ultimately borne by the final consumer.
- **Custom Duty**: This is a tax imposed on goods when they are transported across international borders. The importer usually pays this tax, but the cost is often passed on to consumers.
- **Stamp Duty**: This is a tax on legal documents, usually related to the transfer of assets or property. It is collected by the government through intermediaries.
- **Lottery Tax**: This is a tax on the winnings from lotteries. The tax is usually deducted from the prize money before it is paid out to the winner.
- **Tax on Betting and Gambling**: This is a tax on the revenue generated from betting and gambling activities. It is collected from the operators, who may pass on the cost to the participants.


### Classification of Taxes Cont.

#### Proportional Taxes

- **Definition**: Proportional taxes are taxes where the tax rate remains constant, regardless of the amount of the tax base (income, property value, etc.).
- **Example**: If the tax rate is 10%, everyone pays 10% of their income, whether they earn $10,000 or $100,000.
- **Impact**: This type of tax does not change the relative burden between different income groups.

#### Progressive Taxes

- **Definition**: Progressive taxes are taxes where the tax rate increases as the taxable amount increases.
- **Example**: Income tax systems where higher income earners pay a higher percentage of their income in taxes. For instance, someone earning $50,000 might pay 10%, while someone earning $200,000 might pay 30%.
- **Impact**: This type of tax aims to reduce income inequality by placing a larger burden on those who can afford to pay more.

#### Regressive Taxes

- **Definition**: Regressive taxes are taxes where the tax rate decreases as the taxable amount increases.
- **Example**: Sales taxes can be considered regressive because they take a larger percentage of income from low-income earners than from high-income earners. If both a low-income and a high-income person buy the same $100 item and pay $10 in tax, the $10 is a larger portion of the low-income person’s earnings.
- **Impact**: This type of tax can increase income inequality, as it places a relatively heavier burden on lower-income individuals.

### Resident Status of an Individual 

To decide individual as a resident individual one of below criteria should be fulfill, 

- resides in Sri Lanka. 
- is present in Sri Lanka during the year and that presence falls within a period or periods amounting in aggregate to one hundred and eighty-three days or more in any twelve-month period that commences or ends during the year; 
- is an employee or an official of the Government of Sri Lanka and his spouse is posted abroad during the year; or 
- is an individual who is employed on a Sri Lanka ship, within the meaning of the Merchant Shipping Act, during the period the individual is so employed.

### Resident Status of a Company 

As per the Section 69 of the Act, a Company is deemed to be resident for tax purposes, if; 
- it is incorporated or formed under the laws of Sri Lanka; 
- it is registered or the principal office is in Sri Lanka; or 
- at any time during the year the management and control of the affairs of the company are exercised in Sri Lanka.
